# Succession Order Protocol

## Overview
Guidelines for proper succession to maintain political stability and legitimacy, based on Fan Zhongshan's counsel to King Xuan.

## Core Principle
Succession must follow proper order to maintain legitimacy and prevent rebellion.

## Rules

1. **Order of Succession**
   - Establish the eldest son as heir (not the younger)
   - "Abolishing the elder to establish the younger goes against proper order"

2. **Consequences of Violation**
   - Going against proper order will violate the king's command
   - Violating the king's command requires punishment
   - Creates a dilemma: punish and damage royal authority, or not punish and show commands can be ignored

3. **Chain of Consequences**
   - If the order is not followed, the command loses authority
   - If commands lose authority, governance fails
   - If governance fails while going against proper order, the people will abandon their superiors

4. **Teaching Effect**
   - The ruler's actions teach the people
   - Establishing a younger son teaches people to go against proper order
   - If other states follow this example, royal commands will be blocked everywhere

## Decision Framework
- Proper order: Younger serves elder, subject serves ruler
- Violating this teaches rebellion
- Even if the younger son is more beloved, stability requires following proper order

## Expected Outcomes
- Maintained legitimacy of succession
- Clear expectations for all parties
- Reduced risk of rebellion and instability

## Validation
1. Confirm the eldest son is established as heir following proper order — no younger son is favored regardless of personal preference
2. Verify the chain of consequences is understood: violating order weakens commands, weakened commands undermine governance, failed governance causes abandonment
3. Check the teaching effect — the ruler's succession decision sets a precedent that other states and subjects will follow, so it must model proper order
