# Strategic Petition Framework

A structured approach for writing persuasive petitions that change policy decisions, based on Li Si's "Petition Against Expelling Guests."

## Overview
This framework provides a template for constructing arguments that combine historical precedent, analogical reasoning, and logical analysis to persuade authorities to reverse decisions.

## Structure

### 1. Direct Statement of Position
Begin by clearly stating that the proposed policy is wrong:
- "I have heard that the officials propose expelling guests; I privately consider this a mistake."

### 2. Historical Precedent Section
List past successes that contradict the proposed policy:
- Name specific historical figures who succeeded
- Detail their origins (foreign, outsider status)
- Enumerate their concrete achievements
- Show connection to current prosperity

Example pattern:
- "Ruler X obtained Person Y from Place Z"
- "With them, accomplished [specific achievements]"
- "This continued for [duration]"

### 3. Analogical Reasoning Section
Compare the policy to an obviously illogical parallel:
- List valued items that come from outside
- Show that rejecting outsiders would mean losing these valued things
- Demonstrate inconsistency: "You value foreign goods but reject foreign people"

Key analogy structure:
- If X were true, then Y would follow (reductio ad absurdum)
- Show that Y is obviously undesirable
- Therefore X is wrong

### 4. Logical Cost-Benefit Analysis
Present the logical consequences:
- What is gained by the policy? (nothing)
- What is lost? (talent, loyalty, strength)
- Who benefits? (rivals and enemies)
- Who is harmed? (your own state)

### 5. Principle Appeal
Invoke higher principles:
- "Mount Tai does not reject soil, thus achieves its height"
- "Rivers and seas do not reject streams, thus achieve their depth"
- Connect to universal truths about greatness

### 6. Warning Conclusion
End with stark warning:
- "This is called lending weapons to bandits and giving food to thieves"
- Show that the policy actively helps enemies

## Writing Principles
- Use parallel structure for rhetorical force
- Support every claim with concrete examples
- Address the ruler's self-interest, not abstract morality
- Avoid personal grievance; focus on policy harm

## Expected Outcomes
- Policy is reversed
- Petitioner's position is restored or enhanced
- Precedent is established for future decisions

## Verification
1. Confirm that every claim in the petition is supported by concrete historical examples with named figures and specific achievements.
2. Validate that the analogical reasoning section exposes a clear logical inconsistency in the policy (e.g., valuing foreign goods while rejecting foreign people).
3. Ensure the petition addresses the ruler's self-interest and state benefit, not the petitioner's personal grievance.
