# Proper Rulership Assessment

A framework for evaluating the quality of governance and leadership based on treatment of ministers and management of state affairs.

## Overview
Good rulership can be assessed through specific observable behaviors and outcomes. This framework provides criteria for evaluation.

## Positive Indicators

1. **Treatment of Capable People**
   - Does the ruler value and retain talented individuals?
   - Are capable ministers given appropriate authority?
   - Example: A ruler who has retainers like 子贡, 颜回, 子路, 宰予 demonstrates ability to attract talent

2. **Response to Crisis**
   - Does the ruler take responsibility for failures?
   - Are problems addressed promptly or allowed to fester?

3. **Wealth Distribution**
   - Is state wealth used for public benefit?
   - Are resources distributed to relieve suffering?

4. **Justice and Law**
   - Are laws applied fairly?
   - Do the people feel secure in their persons and property?

## Negative Indicators

1. **Loss of Capable People**
   - Talented individuals leaving or being dismissed
   - Advisors being ignored or punished for honest counsel

2. **Signs of Popular Discontent**
   - People fleeing the territory
   - Increased crime or disorder
   - Complaints about corruption

3. **Ruler's Personal Conduct**
   - Excessive focus on pleasure over governance
   - Neglect of state affairs
   - Trusting flatterers over honest advisors

## Assessment Questions

- "If the ruler were to use this person, would the state prosper or decline?"
- "Do the people speak well of the ruler's governance?"
- "Are neighboring states respectful or contemptuous?"

## Validation
1. Cross-reference positive indicators (talent retention, fair laws) against negative indicators (flight of capable people, popular discontent) — contradictions signal hidden governance failures
2. Verify assessment objectivity by consulting multiple sources: ministers, common people, and neighboring states' envoys
3. Confirm that the ruler's retainer quality (e.g., caliber of advisors like 子贡, 颜回, 子路) reflects genuine talent attraction, not coerced service
