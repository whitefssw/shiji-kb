# Strategic Alliance Formation Against Aggression

## Overview
A procedure for responding to territorial demands from a powerful adversary by using the contested resources to build alliances with the adversary's enemies.

## Steps

1. **Analyze the adversary's position** - Determine if they are attacking from strength or exhaustion
2. **Reject direct concession** - Refuse to give resources directly to the aggressor
3. **Identify the adversary's enemies** - Find states with deep grievances against the aggressor
4. **Offer resources to enemies instead** - Use the contested territory/resources as payment for alliance
5. **Leverage the alliance threat** - The aggressor will likely seek peace once counter-alliance forms
6. **Extract better terms** - Negotiate from the strengthened position

## Key Reasoning
- Giving land to aggressor: strengthens them, weakens you, invites future demands
- Giving land to aggressor's enemy: builds counterweight, deters aggression, may force peace

## Decision Matrix
| Option | Short-term | Long-term |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| Concede to aggressor | Temporary peace | Weakened position, repeated demands |
| Ally with enemy | Potential conflict | Strengthened position, deterrence |

## Expected Outcome
Deterrence of aggression, strengthened diplomatic position, potential for better peace terms

## Validation
1. Verify the identified enemy of the aggressor has genuine grievances and military capability — offering resources to a weak or disinterested party wastes the leverage
2. Confirm the decision matrix has been honestly evaluated: compare short-term and long-term outcomes of concession vs. counter-alliance before committing
3. Assess whether the counter-alliance threat is credible enough to force the aggressor to seek peace — if the alliance is too weak, it provokes escalation rather than deterrence
